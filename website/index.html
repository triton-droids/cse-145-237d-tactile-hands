Tactile Hands

Bryce Hackel*, Sidath, Thomas, Shree, Ali
Triton Droids · UC San Diego · CSE 145/237D · 2026
*Project Lead
Code

A low-cost robotic hand with FSR-based fingertip sensing — calibrated to within 1N and grasping without crushing.

Overview

The goal of this project is to develop a low-cost robotic hand prototype with force-sensing resistors (FSRs) on the fingertips of each finger, capable of grasping and releasing simple objects while actively avoiding crushing them. The sensors will be accurate to within 1N. If time allows, additional input methods such as vision-based finger pose estimation, VR teleoperation, and sim-to-real transfer will be explored to extend the system's capabilities. By the end of the quarter, we will demonstrate a working prototype that can perform basic grasping tasks on a small set of test objects, with the force feedback loop actively modulating grip strength.

Approach

The work is organized into four phases.

Phase 1 — System Design and Component Selection (Weeks 2–3)

Finalize the hand platform (based on AmazingHand), select FSRs, choose the microcontroller (ESP32), and define the electronics architecture. Deliverables: voltage divider circuit design, prototype board layout for the back of the hand, calibration weight selection, and initial simulation environment setup.

Phase 2 — Hardware Build and Sensor Integration (Weeks 3–5)

Assemble the mechanical hand, fabricate and populate the prototype sensor board, and attach FSRs to the fingertips. The ESP32 reads force values over a voltage divider network and transmits them over USB serial. Fake force values are streamed first to validate the data pipeline end-to-end before real sensors are connected. Calibration runs in parallel using 3D-printed weights to map raw readings to Newtons within 1N. The AmazingHand simulation is also brought up.

Phase 3 — Testing, Calibration Validation, and Basic Grasping (Weeks 5–6)

Validate the calibration against known loads and demonstrate a basic grasp-and-release cycle on at least one simple object. The force feedback loop is tested to confirm the system actively avoids crushing the object. This phase culminates in the MVP demo.

Phase 4 — Reach Goals and Documentation (Weeks 7–10)

After the MVP, pursue stretch goals: VR teleoperation via Quest 2, vision-based finger pose estimation, sim-to-real object rotation, and mounting the hand on a robotic arm. Final report and documentation complete this phase.

Minimum Viable Product

A robotic hand prototype with FSRs on each fingertip, calibrated to within 1N accuracy, that demonstrates controlled finger motion and the ability to pick up at least one simple object. Force information must be clearly used to avoid crushing the object.

MVP Goals

  1. Operational hand prototype with tactile sensing — fingers open/close in a controlled way; FSRs are mounted on each fingertip and connected to the prototype board; the ESP32 reads them via voltage dividers and streams readings over USB; structure is mechanically stable.
  2. Calibrated force sensing accurate to within 1N — 3D-printed calibration weights map raw ADC to Newtons; the non-linear curve is fit with a polynomial; procedure is documented and repeatable across all fingers.
  3. Force-aware grasping demonstration — the hand grasps at least one simple object while monitoring fingertip force in real time, and adjusts grip strength to avoid crushing it.
  4. A repeatable test setup — the force-aware behavior is shown multiple times; results are documented with video, force logs, and test notes.

Technological Requirements

Hardware

  • ESP32-S3 DevKit (WROOM-1) — 12-bit ADC, native USB, GPIO for four FSR channels and servo control.
  • Four Pololu FSR #2728 mounted to the AmazingHand fingertips.
  • AmazingHand mechanical platform with four Feetech SCS0009 micro servos.
  • Power: 2S LiPo (7.4V) stepped down to 5V via buck converter, with Schottky diode to block USB backfeed.
  • Prototype board: solderable breadboard on the back of the hand, hosting the ESP32-S3, four 22kΩ voltage dividers, 100nF/10µF decoupling near the ADC, and 0.1" vertical headers for FSR Dupont connections.
  • Reference loads: 3D-printed PLA calibration weights at 0.5N, 1N, 2N.
  • Reference objects: a plastic cup plus simple objects selected during testing.

Software

  • Firmware: Arduino core for ESP32, ADC sampling at 100Hz, USB CDC at 115200 baud, CSV (timestamp + four forces in Newtons).
  • Simulation: MuJoCo or Isaac Sim.
  • Host: ROS 2 on Ubuntu — serial bridge node publishes /tactile/forces, subscribes to /servo/commands.
  • VR: Unity 2022.3 LTS, building the Quest 2 APK from leggedrobotics/unity_ros_teleoperation.
  • Vision: MediaPipe Hands (webcam fallback), ARKit HandPose (iPhone).

Tooling

  • UCSD Makerspace for fabrication and assembly.
  • Bambu Lab A1 Mini for 3D-printed parts and calibration weights.
  • Bench multimeter and oscilloscope.

Testing Infrastructure

  • Calibration rig: a fixture holds each sensor flat and level; weights (0.5N, 1N, 2N, 4N) are stacked sequentially with ADC logged over serial. Each sensor is cycled five times to characterize repeatability.
  • Reference test objects: a cup and additional simple objects, each with a known crush threshold defining grasp pass/fail.
  • Success criteria: error under 1N across the force range; 5/5 consecutive grasp-release cycles per object without exceeding the crush threshold.

Constraints, Risk, and Feasibility

  1. FSR accuracy may be hard to hit within 1N. FSRs are nonlinear and drift with temperature and load cycles. Mitigation: calibration with 3D-printed weights and a nonlinear fit; repeatability testing across multiple cycles.
  2. PCB and wiring may inject noise. Analog FSR signals are sensitive to wiring length, grounding, and Vref stability. Mitigation: proper divider sizing, short analog traces, decoupling caps near the ADC.
  3. The hand may not produce enough force for reliable grasping. Mitigation: start with light, simple objects and basic grasp geometry.
  4. Reach goals depend on multiple subsystems. Mitigation: develop in parallel, integrate only after MVP validation; one owner per reach goal.
  5. Limited time and hardware. Mitigation: reuse available parts; MVP stays the priority; avoid non-essential dependencies for the MVP.

Team

  • Shree — Force sensor calibration: develop and document the calibration procedure, fit the calibration curve, validate ≤1N error, characterize repeatability and drift.
  • Ali — Simulation: bring up the AmazingHand in MuJoCo / Isaac Sim / PyBullet, verify finger kinematics, prepare for sim-to-real. Prior work at the Hao Su Lab (ICLR 2026) on robotic-hand simulation directly informs this role.
  • Sidath — VR teleoperation: build the Quest 2 APK from unity_ros_teleoperation and establish the ROS link. Supports prototyping, actuation, testing, and integration.
  • Thomas — Vision-based finger pose estimation: iPhone app (preferred) or webcam fallback, outputting normalized [0.0–1.0] curl per finger; mapping joint angles to a single per-finger value.
